Why gravity refuses to be quantized

A build-it-up walkthrough for a master's student in particle physics. If you've met Feynman diagrams, the idea of renormalization, and the Einstein–Hilbert action, you have everything you need. New terms are defined as they appear.

What you'll be able to say by the end: why "just quantize gravity like QED" fails at a precise technical step; what each big research program (strings, loop quantum gravity, asymptotic safety, …) is actually trying to fix; why none is finished; and which newer directions look genuinely promising — including one that could be tested on a tabletop.

0. A two-minute warm-up: how a QFT can "fail"

In quantum field theory, a calculation of a process (say, two particles scattering) is a sum of Feynman diagrams. Diagrams with loops contain an integral over the momentum k circulating in the loop, and you have to integrate k all the way up to infinity. Those integrals often come out infinite.

Renormalization is the cure: you realize a few constants in your theory (a mass, a charge) were never the "true" values — they were placeholders. You absorb the infinities into a redefinition of those few constants, fix them once by experiment, and everything else becomes a finite prediction.

Renormalizable theory
All infinities, at every loop order, can be absorbed by adjusting a finite number of constants already in the Lagrangian. QED, the Standard Model. → fully predictive.
Non-renormalizable theory
Each new loop order produces a new kind of infinity needing a new constant. To absorb them all you'd need infinitely many constants, i.e. infinitely many experiments. → predictive power gone at high energy.

The whole drama of quantum gravity is that gravity lands squarely in the second category. The rest of this page is: why, and what to do about it.

1. Where it breaks — and why the culprit is one minus sign in a dimension

You can absolutely start quantizing gravity the naive way. Write the metric as flat spacetime plus a small ripple:

$$ g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}, $$

where ημν is flat (Minkowski) space and hμν is a small field — the graviton. Plug this into the Einstein–Hilbert action and expand. You get a perfectly sensible quantum field: a massless, spin-2 particle, with a propagator and interaction vertices, just like the photon in QED — only with more indices. So far, so good.

Fig 1. Left: the graviton, the quantum of the gravitational field. Right: as soon as you compute a loop, you get an integral over the loop momentum that diverges — and (unlike QED) the divergence keeps changing character at each order.

The smoking gun: just look at the dimensions

Here's the cleanest way to see the problem, using only dimensional analysis. Work in natural units (ℏ=c=1), where everything is measured in powers of energy/mass. The strength of gravity is Newton's constant G, and crucially:

$$ G = \frac{1}{M_{\rm Pl}^2},\qquad M_{\rm Pl}\approx 1.2\times10^{19}\ \text{GeV}. $$

So G has dimensions of (energy)–2. Compare with QED, whose coupling is the fine-structure constant α — a pure number, dimensionless. That single difference is the entire problem. Here's why.

Every extra loop in a gravity diagram brings one more factor of G. But amplitudes are dimensionless-ish quantities; the G has to be compensated by something with dimensions of (energy)+2. At high energy the only scale around is the energy E itself, so each loop comes weighted by

$$ G\,E^2 = \left(\frac{E}{M_{\rm Pl}}\right)^{2}. $$

At everyday energies this is absurdly tiny (gravity is weak!). But as E → MPl it becomes order 1, and higher loops grow faster. The expansion blows up. In QED, by contrast, the coupling is a fixed small number that never grows like this — that's the whole difference between renormalizable and not.

It's not hand-waving — it was computed

People proved this concretely:

  • 1974, 't Hooft & Veltman: pure gravity (no matter) is miraculously finite at one loop — the would-be infinite terms happen to vanish when you use the equations of motion. A famous near-miss. Add any matter (electrons, etc.) and even one loop diverges.
  • 1986, Goroff & Sagnotti: at two loops, pure gravity diverges with an infinity that cannot be absorbed by any constant in Einstein's theory. You'd need to add a brand-new term to the action — and then another at three loops, and so on forever.
Fig 2. Two gravitons come in and out (gold), with a two-loop blob of internal lines (blue). The result is infinite in a way Einstein's theory can't absorb. This is the concrete proof that you cannot treat gravity as "just another QFT."

Three ways to feel the failure

1. Predictions die. Infinitely many constants to fix = infinitely many experiments needed. Near MPl the theory tells you nothing.
2. Probabilities exceed 1. Graviton–graviton scattering grows like (E/MPl)² and eventually predicts probabilities bigger than one — impossible. Something must replace the theory before then. (See Animation 1.)
3. The conceptual headaches (gravity is genuinely different). Even ignoring the infinities, gravity isn't a field living on spacetime — it is spacetime. That breaks the usual QFT setup: there's no fixed background to define "in" and "out" states against, no universal clock (the "problem of time"), and black holes seem to destroy information while carrying an entropy S = A/4G that nobody put in by hand. These are clues, not just obstacles.
strength of graviton scattering ~ (E/MPl)² "probability = 1" ceiling
Animation 1. The strength of gravitational scattering climbs with energy and smashes through the ceiling where probabilities would exceed 1, right around the Planck scale. Every theory below is really a different answer to: "what takes over here?" (For comparison: the same kind of runaway in W-boson scattering correctly predicted the Higgs.)

2. The good news first: gravity as an effective theory

"Non-renormalizable" sounds fatal, but it isn't — it just means the theory has an expiry date in energy. An effective field theory (EFT) is a theory you trust only below some scale, here MPl. Below that scale, treating gravity quantum-mechanically works fine and even makes clean predictions. For example, the quantum-corrected Newton potential between two masses is:

$$ V(r) = -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{r}\Big[\,1 + (\text{tiny GR correction}) + \underbrace{\tfrac{41}{10\pi}\tfrac{G\hbar}{r^2c^3}}_{\text{a real quantum effect}} + \dots\Big]. $$
That 41/10π is a genuine, unambiguous prediction of quantum gravity — no free parameters. So the question is not "is gravity quantum?" Below the Planck scale we already know how to do it. The unsolved problem is what happens at and above MPl — the "UV completion." Everything below is a candidate answer.

candidate 1 String theory

The idea in one sentence: the infinities come from interactions happening at a single point; so make particles into tiny strings, and the interaction gets smeared out, curing the infinities.

In ordinary QFT a particle is a point tracing a line through spacetime (a "worldline"), and two of them meet at a sharp vertex — that sharpness is what makes loop integrals blow up. In string theory a particle is a little loop of string sweeping out a tube (a "worldsheet"), and when two strings merge the join is smooth. A wonderful bonus: one of the vibration modes of a closed string is automatically a massless spin-2 particle — i.e. the graviton drops out for free. Gravity isn't added; it's required.

Animation 2. Left: point particles meeting at a single sharp event — the origin of the infinities. Right: closed strings merging into the smooth "pair of pants." The same process, but with no single point where it happens, which is why string scattering stays finite. This picture is the core selling point of string theory.
Why people love it
  • Loop infinities are gone — finite quantum gravity, with matter and the other forces in the same framework.
  • It correctly counts the entropy S=A/4G of certain black holes from microscopic states (Strominger–Vafa, 1996) — a real triumph.
  • AdS/CFT: in a universe with a particular curved boundary, gravity in the interior is exactly equivalent to an ordinary (non-gravitational) quantum theory on the boundary. This is our single best, fully-consistent definition of quantum gravity, and the main tool for the black-hole information problem.
Why people aren't satisfied
  • It only works in 10 dimensions (and needs supersymmetry). You must "curl up" the extra 6 dimensions — and there are something like 10500 ways to do it (the "landscape"), with no principle picking ours. So it struggles to predict our specific low-energy world.
  • It's cleanest with a fixed spacetime backdrop — arguably against the spirit of GR, where spacetime is dynamical.
  • No agreed way to get our actual expanding (de Sitter) universe out of it.
  • No realistic experiment can reach the energies to test it directly.

candidate 2 Loop quantum gravity

The idea in one sentence: don't add strings or extra dimensions — instead, take Einstein's theory seriously and quantize spacetime geometry itself, and discover that space comes in indivisible chunks.

Here you rewrite GR in variables that look like a gauge theory (similar in spirit to QCD), then apply the rules of quantum mechanics to geometry. The states that come out are networks of lines and nodes called spin networks. The headline result: the operators for area and volume have discrete spectra — there is a smallest possible area (~ the Planck area), just as the hydrogen atom has discrete energy levels. Space is granular at the bottom.

Fig 3. In loop quantum gravity, a region of space is built from a spin network: lines carry definite amounts of area, nodes carry definite amounts of volume. There is no smooth space underneath — it's discrete, like pixels.
Why people love it
  • It treats spacetime as dynamical from the start (no fixed background) — true to GR's spirit.
  • A concrete, vivid prediction: space is discrete, with a smallest area.
  • Applied to cosmology, the Big Bang singularity is replaced by a "bounce" — no infinite density.
  • No extra dimensions, no supersymmetry needed.
Why people aren't satisfied
  • The big one: nobody has convincingly shown that zooming out from these discrete networks gives back smooth, ordinary spacetime and the familiar graviton. Recovering Einstein gravity at large scales — the "continuum limit" — is unsolved.
  • The equation governing the dynamics is ambiguous and hard to solve.
  • Fitting in Standard-Model matter is not as natural as in string theory, and there's no unification of the forces.
  • A literal smallest length can clash with special relativity's length contraction — a worry tightly constrained by observations.

candidate 3 Asymptotic safety

The idea in one sentence: maybe gravity is an ordinary quantum field theory after all — it just isn't tamed by the usual perturbative trick, but by the way its couplings flow with energy.

Coupling "constants" actually change with the energy scale you probe (this is the renormalization group, or RG — it's why the strong coupling weakens at high energy, "asymptotic freedom"). The flow can end at a special point — a fixed point — where the couplings stop changing and the theory stays finite and well-defined up to infinite energy. Weinberg's conjecture: gravity's couplings flow to such a fixed point. If only a finite number of couplings need to be tuned to land on it, you recover predictivity. ("Safe" = no infinities run away; "asymptotic" = as energy → ∞.)

flows captured by the fixed point (well-behaved) a flow that escapes (ill-defined at high energy)
Animation 3. Picture the two main gravitational couplings as a plane, and watch how they change as you raise the energy. Trajectories spiral into the ★ fixed point. A theory sitting on the surface that flows into that point never blows up — that's "asymptotically safe." It's the same idea as asymptotic freedom, but ending at an interacting point instead of at zero.
Why people love it
  • The most economical option: no new particles, dimensions, or symmetries — just QFT done non-perturbatively.
  • Many independent (approximate) calculations keep finding the required fixed point, which is encouraging.
  • It can make predictions — there were even claimed predictions for the Higgs mass from this idea.
Why people aren't satisfied
  • The evidence relies on approximations. The fixed point is found by truncating an infinitely complicated calculation. Whether it's real or an artifact of the approximation is unsettled.
  • Most of the work is done in "imaginary time" (Euclidean); translating back to real time and real probabilities is hard.
  • The higher-derivative terms it needs tend to bring "ghosts" — states with negative probability. Showing the theory is sensible (unitary) is contested.
  • It says little about black holes, singularities, or what spacetime is made of.

candidates 4–6 Build spacetime out of discrete pieces

A family of approaches that, like LQG, bet spacetime is fundamentally discrete or built by counting — but assembled differently.

ApproachThe idea, plainlyWhere it gets stuck
Causal Dynamical Triangulations (CDT) Approximate spacetime by gluing tiny 4D triangle-like blocks, keeping cause-before-effect, and let a computer sum over all the ways to glue them. Remarkably, a smooth 4D universe of the right shape emerges from the simulation. It needs a built-in notion of time-slicing, which sits uneasily with full relativity; and proving it yields real gravitons in the continuum is still in progress.
Causal sets Spacetime = a set of discrete events plus the bare "which event can influence which" relations. Geometry is recovered from just ordering + counting. It even gave a rough early estimate of the tiny cosmological constant. Getting actual dynamics, a usable action, and ordinary matter out of pure ordering is very hard; the theory tends to be stubbornly non-local.
Hořava–Lifshitz gravity Treat space and time on a different footing at high energy (let time scale differently from space). This simple change makes gravity renormalizable by the usual power-counting. It sacrifices the equal treatment of space and time (Lorentz symmetry) at high energy, and has trouble getting that symmetry back at low energy where experiments demand it — plus an unwanted extra mode.

candidate 7 Maybe gravity shouldn't be quantized at all

The idea in one sentence: perhaps the metric isn't fundamental — it's an average, like temperature or pressure — and quantizing it would be a category error, like trying to "quantize" the temperature of a gas instead of the molecules.

The striking result here is Jacobson's (1995): if you assume every small observer sees a horizon with entropy S=A/4G and demand the basic thermodynamic relation "heat = temperature × entropy change" hold everywhere, then Einstein's equations pop out automatically — as if they were the "equation of state" of some underlying stuff. Related modern work shows that in AdS/CFT, the shape of spacetime is built up from the quantum entanglement of the boundary theory — "entanglement is the glue of spacetime."

The catch: these arguments strongly suggest gravity is emergent, but they don't tell you what the underlying "molecules of spacetime" actually are, and most don't yet predict new numbers. And whether the metric has its own quantum jiggle is exactly what we'd like to measure (next section).

8. Directions that look genuinely promising

The freshest progress isn't a brand-new "theory of everything" — it's a set of sharper tools and one possible experiment. These are also the most accessible places for a young particle physicist to jump in.

(a) "Gravity = the square of a force we understand"

A startling discovery in scattering-amplitude calculations: the formula for a graviton interaction looks like the formula for a gluon (strong-force) interaction squared. This "double copy" makes horrendous gravity calculations tractable and hints that gravity is built out of ordinary gauge theory rather than being fundamental. We don't yet fully understand why it works — cracking that could reshape how we think about the metric.

(b) Black holes might not destroy information after all

Hawking's old answer: information lost "Page curve": information preserved
Animation 4. Track the entanglement entropy of a black hole's outgoing radiation. Hawking's calculation says it rises forever (information destroyed — a paradox, since quantum mechanics forbids that). The unitary answer (the "Page curve," green) must rise then fall back to zero. Since ~2019, new gravity calculations involving "islands" and "replica wormholes" reproduce the green curve — recovering the good answer from gravity itself, without needing the full theory. A major breakthrough, though making it work for real 4D black holes is unfinished.

(c) Holography for a realistic universe (celestial holography)

AdS/CFT is powerful but lives in a universe with the wrong (curved-boundary) shape. A young, fast-moving effort tries to build a similar "boundary description" for ordinary flat space, by organizing particle collisions using the symmetries seen infinitely far away. It connects to "soft theorems" — facts about very-low-energy photons and gravitons you already meet in QFT — and is wide open for new work.

(d) A tabletop test of whether gravity is quantum at all

Here's the experiment to watch (the "BMV" proposal). Take two tiny masses, put each into a quantum superposition of two places at once, and let them sit near each other so they interact only through gravity. Quantum mechanics says: if the gravitational interaction can entangle the two masses, then gravity itself must be quantum (a purely classical force can't create entanglement). This is a real, if extremely hard, near-future experiment — and it could tell us whether the metric must be quantized without ever reaching the Planck energy. Hugely under-appreciated.

(e) Getting the different camps to talk

Some of the best low-hanging fruit is just connecting programs that rarely compare notes: e.g. checking whether the "fixed point" of asymptotic safety and the smooth universe that emerges from CDT simulations are describing the same thing; or — fitting, since this very file lives inside the GAMBIT global-fitting code — using data-fitting machinery to confront gravity-plus-new-physics models with cosmology and gravitational-wave data.

The take-home in one paragraph. Quantizing gravity is easy at low energies — it's a perfectly good effective theory that even predicts small quantum corrections to Newton's law. The crisis is purely at high energy (the Planck scale), where infinities pile up uncontrollably because Newton's constant carries the "wrong" dimensions, plus a set of deep puzzles because gravity is spacetime rather than a field on it. String theory gives the most complete cure but at the price of extra dimensions and a vast, non-predictive landscape; loop quantum gravity and the discrete approaches keep four dimensions and background independence but can't yet rebuild smooth spacetime; asymptotic safety is the most conservative but rests on approximations. The most exciting recent oxygen comes from sharper tools (the gravity-as-gauge-squared "double copy," the island/Page-curve rescue of black-hole information, flat-space holography) — and, remarkably, from a tabletop experiment that may soon tell us whether gravity is quantum at all.
